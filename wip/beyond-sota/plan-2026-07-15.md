## B. Unit 1 — PINO (priority; specified in full)

This is the priority because it is the direct, public, technical question we were asked, and because the docs
already promise it.

### B.1 What gets built

A **new** case `bench-darcy-pino` (`method="operator-pino"`), leaving the existing data-driven FNO case intact
as the baseline. Same Darcy family, same grid, same architecture, same seed: **only the loss changes**, so the
comparison is honest.

### B.2 The scheme (paper Section 3, transcribed)

**Phase 1, pre-training.** Loss = data loss on `n_labels` solved instances + `lambda` x PDE loss, where the
PDE loss also runs on **virtual instances** `a' ~ mu` drawn fresh every step. Generating `a'` costs a Gaussian
filter and a threshold: **no solve**. This is Algorithm 1, and it is what makes the label budget the free
variable.

**Phase 2, test-time optimization.** Fine-tune the pre-trained operator on a single queried instance with
`L_pde + alpha * L_op`, `L_op = || G_theta_i(a) - G_theta_0(a) ||^2` the anchor loss against the frozen
pre-trained prediction. Implemented in `model/pino.py::test_time_optimize`, returning a per-step history so
the app can SHOW the residual falling rather than assert it.

### B.3 The residual on a grid output (paper Section 3.3) — VERIFIED

`model/pino.py::darcy_residual_fd` is the finite-volume divergence form with **harmonic-mean** face
conductivities (the correct face average for a discontinuous coefficient; an arithmetic mean over-conducts
across a jump). Verified before any case was built on it:

| Input | rms residual | Expected |
|---|---|---|
| the reference solution u | **3.6e-14** | ~0 (machine precision) |
| u x 1.10 | **1.0e-01** | ~10%, the operator being linear |
| u = 0 | **1.000** | exactly f = 1 |

So the residual is the discrete Darcy operator, confirmed in both directions (a residual that is accidentally
zero for everything cannot pass this).

**Why finite difference and not the FFT.** The paper offers a spectral route at `O(n log n)` but warns that
"spectral methods require smoothness and uniform grids". Darcy here is non-periodic (u = 0 on the boundary)
with a piecewise-constant coefficient. Measured on a reference field, the spectral Laplacian route gives
rms |-a_mean * lap(u) - f| = **6.0**, i.e. six times the source term: the periodicity assumption fails
exactly as warned. `spectral_laplacian` is kept as the documented cross-check, not the default. The
non-periodic spectral fix is Fourier continuation (FC-PINO, Maust et al. arXiv:2211.15960), not implemented.

### B.4 What the case MEASURES (our own numbers, not the paper's)

1. **Data-reduction curve** (the honest small-scale version of the paper's data-efficiency claim):
   held-out relative-L2 vs `n_labels` in {0, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64}, for data-only FNO vs PINO, everything else
   identical. At `n_labels = 0` the data-only operator has no signal at all; PINO still has the equation.
2. **Test-time optimization**: residual and error before vs after fine-tuning a held-out instance, with the
   anchor loss on, plus wall-clock.
3. **Training cost**, because "PINNs train too long" is the critique: seconds per lane, reported.

#### SPIKE RESULT (measured 2026-07-15, before building the case)

Same architecture (FNO2d width 20, modes 10, 4 layers), same seed, same 150 epochs, same held-out test set of
16 instances on a 32x32 grid. **Only the loss differs.** Held-out mean relative-L2:

| n_labels | FNO (data only) | PINO (data + physics) | PINO better by |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | 0.8863 | **0.4715** | 47% |
| 4 | 0.2662 | **0.1943** | 27% |
| 8 | 0.1894 | **0.1711** | 10% |
| 16 | 0.1442 | **0.1295** | 10% |
| 32 | 0.1091 | **0.0953** | 13% |
| 64 | 0.0855 | **0.0569** | 33% |

PINO wins at every budget. Reading it honestly:

- At **zero labels** the data-only operator is untrained by definition (0.886 is a random operator); PINO
  reaches 0.47 from the equation alone. That is the qualitative point, not a good absolute number.
- The data-efficiency crossing is modest: PINO at 4 labels (0.194) roughly equals FNO at 8 labels (0.189),
  so about **2x fewer labels** at the low end. Higher up the curve PINO at n is a little worse than FNO at
  2n, so the honest claim is "PINO is consistently better at equal labels, and worth roughly 1.5-2x labels at
  the low end", NOT a blanket data-efficiency multiplier.
- Cost: the PINO lane roughly doubles training time (it adds a residual on labelled data plus a virtual
  batch every step). This must be reported, not hidden, since training cost is the whole critique.

This is enough to build the case on: the mechanism is real and measured on our own grid.

#### WHAT THE FULL BUILDS ACTUALLY SHOWED (the honest record of three configurations)

The spike above used 150 epochs on a 64-instance pool. The shipped case uses 80 epochs on a 128-instance
pool, and there the picture is **not** a clean sweep. Held-out relative-L2, PINO vs data-only FNO, "better"
positive means PINO wins:

| n_labels | (1) lambda = 1, soft BC | (2) balanced lambda, soft BC | (3) balanced lambda, HARD BC |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | +45.0% | +45.0% | (see build) |
| 8 | **-41.1%** | **-126.4%** | still losing (spike: -233%) |
| 32 | -0.5% | **-68.9%** | **+37%** (spike) |
| 128 | +33.9% | +19.2% | (see build) |

Three findings, all measured, none of them tuned-to-win:

1. **The gradient pathology is real and was in our own implementation.** At initialisation the two terms are
   balanced in VALUE (L_data 1.07, L_pde 1.00) but their GRADIENT norms differ by **38.6x**
   (3.28 vs 0.085), so at lambda = 1 the equation supplied ~2.6% of the update. Measured, not assumed.
2. **Gradient-norm balancing alone made it WORSE** in the mid-label regime (-41% -> -126% at 8 labels).
   Correcting the weight without correcting the constraint just lets an under-converged physics term
   dominate.
3. **The missing piece was the boundary condition.** The residual is interior-only and the Darcy solution is
   unique only WITH its boundary condition, so a strong physics weight can drive the interior residual to
   zero around the WRONG boundary values. Enforcing u = 0 exactly (the repo's `hard-constraints` idiom,
   applied to an operator) flips 32 labels from losing to **winning by 37%**.
4. **The low-label regime (8) still loses**, and the reason is the critique itself: the physics term
   converges SLOWLY, so when it is weighted to matter it drags the operator toward a not-yet-converged
   physics solution, which is worse than simply fitting 8 labels. This is the "PINNs take very long to train"
   objection, reproduced and measured inside our own operator. The case must SHOW this, not hide it.

**Consequence for the case's claim.** The honest statement is NOT "PINO beats FNO". It is: the equation
replaces labels where labels are absent (zero-label), and helps once there are enough labels to keep the
physics term honest, but at a small non-zero budget an under-trained physics term is worse than nothing. That
is a more useful result than a clean win, and it is ours, measured.

#### THE SHIPPED CONFIGURATION (hard BC + gradient-balanced lambda), 2026-07-15

| n_labels | PINO | data-only FNO | PINO better | lambda | t_pino | t_fno |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | 0.6885 | 1.0452 | **+34.1%** | 1.0 | 26 s | 0 s |
| 8 | 0.4708 | 0.1696 | **-177.6%** | 1.9 | 83 s | 27 s |
| 32 | **0.0784** | 0.1433 | **+45.3%** | 0.5 | 250 s | 115 s |
| 128 | **0.0371** | 0.0715 | **+48.1%** | 1.6 | 766 s | 377 s |

The hard boundary constraint was the decisive fix: 32 labels went from -0.5% (soft, lambda=1) and -68.9%
(soft, balanced) to **+45.3%**, and 128 from +33.9%/+19.2% to **+48.1%**. Three of four budgets now favour
PINO clearly.

**The 8-label failure is real, reproducible across all three configurations, and is the critique itself.**
Note lambda = 1.9 there, so this is NOT the balancer over-weighting physics. PINO lands at 0.471, close to
its own zero-label result (0.689): with only 8 labels the data term cannot pin the solution, so the outcome
is dominated by a physics term that has not yet converged after 80 epochs. In other words, the equation is
the right signal but a SLOW one, which is exactly what the commenter said about PINN training cost, now
measured inside our own operator. Ship it as a finding, with the training seconds beside it, rather than
hiding the budget.

**Cost, stated plainly:** the PINO lane costs roughly 2x the data-only lane per budget (766 s vs 377 s at
128 labels). Any "cheaper" claim would be about LABELS (solver calls avoided), never about training time.

#### A separate measurement that constrains what this product may claim

Measured on this machine: the classical reference solve at 32x32 takes **42.4 ms**, while the exported
operator's ONNX inference takes **35-39 ms**. At the catalogue's grid size the neural operator has
**essentially no speed advantage** over the sparse direct solve, and the reference assembly is an unoptimised
Python loop over `lil_matrix`, so 42 ms overstates the classical side. The many-query speed argument for
operators is real in the literature but is NOT demonstrated at our grid size. Do not claim it until we run a
grid where it is true.

### B.5 The honesty constraints for this unit

- The FD residual uses the **same stencil** as the reference solver. Driving it to zero *is* solving the
  reference's discrete system, so "PINO matches the reference" is not a discretisation-accuracy result. What
  the physics term buys, and all we may claim, is **data efficiency**. This must be said in the case doc.
- Do not quote the paper's headline numbers (20x error / 25x speedup on Kolmogorov, 400x vs the pseudo-spectral
  solver, 3000x on Darcy inversion) as ours. Cite them as the paper's, report ours separately.
- A classical sparse direct solve still beats all of this for a single well-posed forward instance. The
  operator's argument is many-query amortisation, not one-shot speed.

## C. Unit 2 — the geometric / structure-preserving family (specified, pending research)

The gap is real and the target is clear: `dyn-double-pendulum` currently integrates a plain residual PINN, so
**energy is not conserved by construction**. The double pendulum released from rest has an exactly conserved
total energy E = T + V, which makes energy drift a clean, measurable, honest discriminator.

Planned ladder on the same system, so the contrast is like-for-like:
- **naive**: the existing residual PINN (what we ship today)
- **adapted**: a structure-preserving model (Hamiltonian/symplectic; exact form pending the research pass)
- **metric**: |E(t) - E(0)| / E(0) over the window, alongside the existing leave-time against RK45

To be finalised from the survey: which of HNN (Greydanus et al.), LNN (Cranmer et al.), DeLaN (Lutter et al.),
SympNets or a symplectic-integrator-structured network is the best single tier to ship first, and whether a
second, non-chaotic system is needed to show conservation cleanly (chaos limits how much a trajectory metric
can say).

## D. Unit 3 — applied problems (specified, pending research)

**Transient groundwater with a pumping well** is the first target: it is a canonical applied problem, it has
an analytic anchor (Theis), and it is the exact demonstration a practitioner posted publicly using PINO. The
parameters from that post are a reasonable starting point (domain 1000 m x 100 m, fixed head 100 m left /
90 m right, no-flux top and bottom, well at (500, 50) m, K = 10 m/day, S = 0.2, small recharge, compared to a
finite-difference reference at t = 40 days), but every value and the well-singularity treatment must be
checked against the literature before use, not copied from a post.

Remaining applied candidates come from the mining and subsurface survey angles.
