## What operator learning is

A standard PINN solves **one** boundary-value problem: it fits a single field $u(x,t)$ for one fixed
source term, one set of boundary conditions, one diffusivity. Change the source or the coefficient and
you must retrain from scratch. That is fine for a one-off inverse problem, but it is the wrong tool for a
**many-query** workload — design sweeps, uncertainty propagation, real-time control — where you must
evaluate the solution for thousands of different inputs.

Operator learning attacks the many-query problem directly. Instead of approximating a function
$u : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}$, it approximates the **solution operator** of a PDE family

$$
\mathcal{G} : \mathcal{A} \longrightarrow \mathcal{U}, \qquad a \longmapsto u,
$$

a mapping between two **infinite-dimensional function spaces**: the input space $\mathcal{A}$ of
coefficients / sources / boundary data, and the output space $\mathcal{U}$ of solution fields. Once
$\mathcal{G}$ is learned from a dataset of input–output pairs $\{(a_i, u_i)\}_{i=1}^N$, a new input
function $a$ is mapped to its solution in a **single forward pass** — no optimisation loop, no retraining.
This is the "train once, infer instantly over a whole family" property that makes operators a surrogate
technology rather than a solver.

The price is a different bargain than a PINN's. A PINN needs only the PDE (no labelled data), but produces
one solution. An operator typically needs a **dataset of solved instances** (generated by a classical
solver, or by physics constraints in the case of PINO), and in return generalises across the family — but
**only within the training distribution**. The three architectures below are the load-bearing members of
this group, in the order they appear in the literature.

---

## Contrast with single-instance PINNs

| | **Single-instance PINN** | **Neural operator (DeepONet / FNO / PINO)** |
|---|---|---|
| Learns | one field $u(x,t)$ | the operator $\mathcal{G}: a \mapsto u$ over a family |
| Inputs vary? | no — retrain per instance | yes — new $a$ inferred in one forward pass |
| Training data | none required (PDE only) | dataset of solved instances (PINO: + physics) |
| Many-query cost | $N \times$ full training | one training + $N$ cheap inferences |
| Generalisation risk | n/a (single problem) | **bounded by training distribution** (OOD fails) |
| Best for | inverse problems, sparse-data assimilation | design sweeps, UQ, control, real-time surrogates |

The honest framing PINN-Lab takes (dossier §1): operators are included **where they are the right tool —
parametric surrogates** — with their failure modes called out, not as a PINN replacement. For a single
well-posed forward solve, a good classical solver still wins on speed and accuracy.

## 1. DeepONet (+ POD-DeepONet, MIONet)

**What it is.** DeepONet is the first deep-learning operator architecture with a universal-approximation
guarantee, built on the operator approximation theorem of Chen & Chen (1995): a neural network with a
single hidden layer can approximate any nonlinear continuous operator. DeepONet realises this with two
sub-networks — a **branch net** that encodes the input function $a$ sampled at a fixed set of $m$ "sensor"
locations $\{x_1,\dots,x_m\}$, and a **trunk net** that encodes the output query coordinate $\xi$ at which
the solution is evaluated. Their outputs are combined by an **inner product**, so the predicted value of
the output function at $\xi$ is

$$
\mathcal{G}(a)(\xi) \;\approx\; \sum_{k=1}^{p} \underbrace{b_k\!\big(a(x_1),\dots,a(x_m)\big)}_{\text{branch}}
\; \underbrace{t_k(\xi)}_{\text{trunk}} \; + \; b_0 ,
$$

where $\{b_k\}$ are the $p$ branch outputs and $\{t_k\}$ the $p$ trunk outputs. The branch learns
coefficients of a data-driven basis $\{t_k\}$ that the trunk learns simultaneously. Because the trunk takes
**continuous** query coordinates, DeepONet evaluates the output field anywhere — it is mesh-free on the
output side. Two strong variants: **POD-DeepONet** fixes the trunk basis to POD/PCA modes of the training
solutions (only the branch is trained, often more accurate and cheaper), and **MIONet** generalises the
inner-product structure to operators with **multiple input functions** (multiple branch nets, one per
input), with its own approximation theorem.

**When to use it.** When the input is a function naturally observed at fixed sensors (a source field on a
grid, a boundary profile, a permeability map), and you want pointwise output queries. DeepONet is the
production-grade choice in DeepXDE and is flexible about input/output discretisation. Its weakness relative
to FNO is that the sensor locations are fixed at training time and resolution-invariance is not built in
(POD-DeepONet partly addresses this).

**Key equation.** The branch–trunk inner product above.

**Canonical reference.** Lu, Jin & Karniadakis, *Learning nonlinear operators via DeepONet based on the
universal approximation theorem of operators*, **Nature Machine Intelligence 3, 218–229 (2021)**;
arXiv:1910.03193 (submitted Oct 2019). DOI: [10.1038/s42256-021-00302-5](https://doi.org/10.1038/s42256-021-00302-5).
POD-DeepONet/comparisons: Lu et al., CMAME 2022 (arXiv:2111.05512). MIONet: Jin, Meng & Lu, SIAM J. Sci.
Comput. 2022 (arXiv:2202.06137).

**Framework.** **DeepXDE** (`dde.nn.DeepONet` / `dde.nn.DeepONetCartesianProd`; production path) — also
PhysicsNeMo and PINA.

## 2. FNO — Fourier Neural Operator (+ TFNO/SFNO/GINO)

**What it is.** The Fourier Neural Operator parameterises the operator as a sequence of **kernel-integral
layers**, and evaluates each integral as a **convolution in the spatial domain** — which, by the
convolution theorem, is a **pointwise multiplication in Fourier space**. Each FNO layer updates the hidden
function $v_\ell$ by

$$
v_{\ell+1}(x) \;=\; \sigma\!\Big( W v_\ell(x) \;+\; \big(\mathcal{K} v_\ell\big)(x) \Big),
\qquad
\big(\mathcal{K} v_\ell\big)(x) \;=\; \mathcal{F}^{-1}\!\Big( R_\phi \cdot \mathcal{F}(v_\ell) \Big)(x),
$$

where $\mathcal{F}$ / $\mathcal{F}^{-1}$ are the (FFT-implemented) forward and inverse Fourier transforms,
$R_\phi$ is a learned complex weight tensor that acts on the lowest $k_{\max}$ Fourier modes (higher modes
are truncated), and $W$ is a pointwise (residual) linear map. Because the kernel is learned in **mode
space**, not on a fixed grid, the same trained FNO can be evaluated on a **finer or coarser mesh than it was
trained on** — this is its signature **discretisation-invariance / zero-shot super-resolution** property.
On the target PDE family FNO runs up to **three orders of magnitude faster** than a classical solver.
Variants: **TFNO** (Tucker-factorised weights, fewer parameters), **SFNO** (spherical harmonics for
global/climate domains), **GINO** (graph encoder/decoder so the spectral core can handle **irregular 3D
geometry**).

**When to use it.** When the input and output live on (or can be mapped to) a **regular grid** and the
operator is reasonably **translation-equivariant** — Darcy flow over a permeability field, Navier–Stokes
turbulence, advection–diffusion families. It is the reference operator for `bench-darcy-operator`.

**Honest limitations (state these on the Benchmark page).** The fixed mode truncation $k_{\max}$ means FNO
**cannot synthesise frequencies it never saw** — it under-resolves sharp/high-frequency features outside
the training band. **Out-of-distribution boundary conditions or parameters error badly**, because the
operator only interpolates within its training distribution. On nonlinear systems it can exhibit
**artificial energy dissipation** over long rollouts. Plain FNO needs a regular grid (use GINO for
unstructured geometry). These are intrinsic, not tuning bugs — operators are bounded by their training set,
which is why PINN-Lab makes **OOD validation against a classical solver mandatory** for every operator case.

**Key equation.** The spectral-convolution layer above.

**Canonical reference.** Li, Kovachki, Azizzadenesheli, Liu, Bhattacharya, Stuart & Anandkumar,
*Fourier Neural Operator for Parametric Partial Differential Equations*, **ICLR 2021**; arXiv:2010.08895
(submitted Oct 2020). Library paper: Kossaifi et al., *A Library for Learning Neural Operators*,
arXiv:2412.10354.

**Framework.** **neuraloperator** (`neuralop.models.FNO` / `TFNO` / `SFNO` / `GINO`; the FNO reference
library) — also PhysicsNeMo.

## 3. PINO — Physics-Informed Neural Operator

**What it is.** PINO is FNO trained with **two losses at once**: a data loss on solved instances **and** a
**PDE-residual loss** evaluated on the operator's own output. Crucially, the physics term is imposed at a
**higher resolution** than the (coarse, expensive-to-generate) training data, exploiting FNO's
discretisation-invariance to regularise the fine-scale behaviour for free. The training objective is

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{PINO}}
= \underbrace{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\big\| \mathcal{G}_\theta(a_i) - u_i \big\|^2}_{\text{data loss (coarse)}}
\;+\;
\lambda \,\underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{a}\,\big\| \mathcal{R}\big(a,\,\mathcal{G}_\theta(a)\big) \big\|^2}_{\text{PDE residual (fine)}},
$$

where $\mathcal{R}$ is the PDE residual operator (the strong-form differential equation set to zero) applied
to the operator output, and $\lambda$ balances the two terms. The residual is computed efficiently in
Fourier space (exact spectral derivatives) or by a numerical scheme. This combination buys two things:
**zero-shot super-resolution with no accuracy loss** (the physics constraint enforces correctness on the
fine grid the data never resolved), and **far less data hunger** — in the limit PINO can train with **only**
the PDE constraint and **no labelled data**, succeeding on problems (e.g. Kolmogorov / chaotic flows) where
a plain single-instance PINN's optimisation fails. PINO is the "operator + governing equations" recipe: it
inherits FNO's many-query speed and adds the PINN's physics prior. **FC-PINO** extends it to
non-periodic domains via Fourier continuation (FFT assumes periodicity, which leaks error otherwise).

> **What WE measured, which is not a clean win** (`bench-darcy-pino`, 2026-07-15). The paragraph above is the
> literature's claim. On our own 32x32 Darcy grid the physics term helps where labels are absent (zero
> labels: 0.59 vs a data-only operator that is untrained by definition) and in the data-rich regime, but at
> **8 and 32 labels our PINO lane is worse than the data-only FNO** (0.344 vs 0.152; 0.177 vs 0.105). The
> cause is measured, not guessed: the gradient-norm balancer drove lambda to 29.3 at 8 labels, leaving the
> data term ~3% of the update while the physics term was still far from converged. So do **not** read "far
> less data hunger" as something this repo has demonstrated. See `wip/beyond-sota/plan-2026-07-15.md` §B.4
> for all three configurations and the repair plan.

**When to use it.** When you want an FNO surrogate but have **few solved instances**, or need the surrogate
to be **physically consistent at resolutions beyond the training grid**, or want to push toward the
data-free regime. It is exercised by **`bench-darcy-pino`**, the companion case to the data-driven
`bench-darcy-operator`: same Darcy family, same architecture, same seed, same epochs, so the only difference
is the loss and the data-vs-physics trade-off is measured rather than asserted.

**Honest limitations.** PINO inherits FNO's OOD fragility and (where periodic FFTs are used) the
periodicity assumption; the residual loss adds the usual PINN loss-balancing difficulty ($\lambda$ tuning,
gradient-scale imbalance). It is heavier to train than plain FNO because of the extra fine-grid residual.

**Key equation.** The combined data + (fine-grid) PDE-residual loss above.

**Canonical reference.** Li, Zheng, Kovachki, Jin, Chen, Liu, Azizzadenesheli & Anandkumar,
*Physics-Informed Neural Operator for Learning Partial Differential Equations*, **ACM/IMS Journal of Data
Science 1(3), 2024**; arXiv:2111.03794 (submitted Nov 2021). DOI:
[10.1145/3648506](https://doi.org/10.1145/3648506). FC-PINO: Maust et al., arXiv:2211.15960.

**Framework.** **neuraloperator** / the zongyi-li `PINO` repo — also PhysicsNeMo.

## How this maps into PINN-Lab

**Exercised by `bench-darcy-operator`** (canonical group; coverage map A row 7, methods rows 18–20). The case
learns the **Darcy solution operator** over a family of permeability fields:

$$
-\,\nabla\!\cdot\!\big(a(x)\,\nabla u(x)\big) \;=\; f(x), \qquad x \in \Omega,
\quad u|_{\partial\Omega} = 0,
$$

so the operator is $\mathcal{G}: a(x) \mapsto u(x)$ over a distribution of coefficient fields $a(x)$.

**What is actually implemented, and where** (corrected 2026-07-15 — this section previously claimed one case
exercised all three methods, which was not true; see `wip/beyond-sota/audit-claims-vs-engine-2026-07-15.md`):

| Method | Status | Case |
|---|---|---|
| **FNO** | **shipped** — a real 2D Fourier Neural Operator trained on the Darcy family and exported to ONNX | `bench-darcy-operator` (data-driven only) |
| **PINO** | **shipped** — the equation enters the operator's own loss, plus virtual instances and test-time optimization with the anchor loss | `bench-darcy-pino` |
| **DeepONet** | **NOT implemented.** Documented above as a method; no case exercises it yet. | — |

The two Darcy cases are deliberately a matched pair: same family, same architecture, same seed, same epochs,
so the only difference between them is the loss.

**Pipeline lane.** Operators are a **precompute-lane** technology, not single-instance solves. Per dossier
§3.1, `bench-darcy-operator` runs on **neuraloperator** in `.venv-pipeline`, which assembles the parametric
dataset (coefficient family $a$, classical-solver solutions $u$), trains the FNO/PINO, **validates OOD
against a classical Darcy solver** (mandatory — operators are bounded by their training distribution), then
exports a standard `torch.onnx.export` of the operator (a plain `torch.nn.Module`). DeepONet exports the bare
branch+trunk module the same way.

**Live lane.** The exported operator ONNX runs under **onnxruntime-web**: the input is a coefficient/source
function (an `[N, …]` tensor), the output is the predicted field — instant many-query inference in the
browser, which is precisely the property single-instance PINNs lack. The replay artifact bakes a
representative sweep of inputs for first paint and the offline fallback.

**Honesty bar.** The Benchmark page for this case **must show the OOD-error column** and label the FNO/PINO
failure modes (fixed-mode high-frequency limit, OOD-BC error, energy dissipation) — operators are presented
as the right tool for parametric surrogates, **never as a PINN or FEM/FVM replacement** (dossier §1).

**Documentation cross-links.** `docs/frameworks/neuraloperator/` (the operator-lane engine guide, with the
failure modes), `docs/frameworks/deepxde/` (DeepONet path), `docs/cases/bench-darcy-operator.md` (governing
equations, viz, benchmark vs. classical solver).
